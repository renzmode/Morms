Embers and Codes: A journey through the screen

By Nio Hameed Negrete & Tasha Aamirah Penetrante

The largest of flames most typically start with the faintest of embers. A small flicker of a flame has a magnitude of possibilities.

Fires are typically used as a symbol of passion and strength, the burning sensation within oneself to continue to strive for their goals.

Click "Read Full Article" to continue reading this inspiring journey...

The largest of flames most typically start with the faintest of embers. A small flicker of a flame has a magnitude of possibilities.

A possibility for this ember to grow, expand, and glow even brighter. As long as its light remains, it will continue to guide those lost in the dark and continue to fan the flames of one within. This feeble ember goes unextinguished and continues to grow infinitely.

Fires are typically used as a symbol of passion and strength, the burning sensation within oneself to continue to strive for their goals. A burning passion that goes unextinguished by life's obstacles and barriers. These inner fires are brightest within students, even as small embers. A small passion is starting to grow and growing bigger and brighter by the second.

For many students, the word "coding" sparks a mix of curiosity, intimidation, and fear. It suggests long hours in front of a screen, puzzling through complex lines of text that seem to speak in an alien language. But for the student learners who ventured into the iamtheCODE platform, what began as uncertainty transformed into a journey of discoveryone that extended far beyond programming.

As a global movement, iamtheCODE aims to empower young peopleespecially girlsthrough STEAMD (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, Mathematics, and Design) education. The iamtheCODE program is the first-ever African-led initiative that aims to empower women and girls to become coders by 2030. Its partnership with the Department of Education was officially formalized on September 5, 2024, marking the start of a new era of women's empowerment in the Philippines.

The Pioneer Journey

The program is a 12-week course with several learning competencies. Its modules are designed to develop not only technical literacy but also communication, creativity, leadership, and problem-solving. At Marcial O. Raola Memorial School (MORMS), the program has sparked new confidence, new aspirations, and new stories of growth. It officially started in March of 2025, when the school gathered a variety of participants from 10th to 12th grade levels.

For the participants, the program was like a whole new experience, still navigating the program's ins and outs. As the pioneer batch of this program, their journey was truly like an adventure. Most of them had limited prior knowledge of the competencies of the program, but this only fueled their desire to learn and to grow with iamtheCODE.

Overcoming Challenges

Their journey was truly not an easy one, as they were grasping this completely new environment while juggling their own responsibilities as students. The 12-week online course consisted of several video lectures for the participants to properly grasp the topic and several exercises and assessments. The program was really not for the faint of heart, as the participants endured hours of study time, almost entire days completing a module, and learning new lingo that seemed alien to them.

The biggest problem that most encountered in the program was technical issues here and there. Most didn't know how to even access the program; some had the infinite struggle of logging in to their account, but once these first few weeks of adjustments were settled, the program became a little lighter for the participants. Most were already starting their journeys with their heads held high and eager to learn.

Student Reflections:

"My first impression of the program is kind of boring because I couldn't understand the platform at all," one participant admitted.

Another described it as "a high mountain to climb on."

Anne shared that she was intimidated simply because coding wasn't something she was used to.

Another participant even confessed they initially skimmed through videos without paying much attention, thinking they might be "hour-long and boring."

The Transformation

The first few modules were some that the participants enjoyed the most. Phase 1, or modules 1-6 of the program, was solely code-based and focused on the main essence of the program, to teach the participants to code in different languages such as HTML, Python, CSS, and Javascript. Yet the long lectures of endless video lessons sometimes became a drag for most. But most can't deny that they really loved the coding exercises where you can really feel like you're a pro coder creating a new program or platform, whereas the participants were only making sample blogs through code.

Coding, for most, seems like a very broad field, for expert techy kids, but with the iamtheCODE program, coding has become an avenue for all students to gain knowledge in coding and creating programs, which they may benefit from. The journey in completing the program may have been difficult with late nights of iamtheCODE study sessions and using these lessons to create programs in school, but most of the participants were deeply thankful for joining the program.

Beyond Coding

Those 7 months of coding, learning, and improving one's skills were full of both ups and downs. Most admit to having been intimidated by the program at first, but after completing the entire program, they realized the true beauties of it. They were continuously driven by their desire to complete the program and their desire to acquire new skills that they may use for their future.

iamtheCODE taught more than just codes; it taught about leadership, communication, and good well-being for all. The program is a doorway for students, and especially girls, to learn something that they once thought they could never learn. It broke the stigma that coding is only for the techy and nerdy ones; it taught those participants the true essence of coding. Truthfully, anyone can code with the proper will and desire to learn, and with the 37 completers with completely different characters, profiles, and interests, iamtheCODE has successfully done its job.

The Proper Drive

The proper drive and passion for a certain discipline is the only thing one needs in order to succeed in fields such as coding and ICT. From regular students with no knowledge of simple coding to intermediate coders with the proper knowledge to create programs, platforms, and initiatives through codes, iamtheCODE has opened a door of infinite opportunities for its learners.

Just like an inextinguishable fire, the passion and determination of the student coders continues to grow and flourish. Their once embers of passion for coding have grown into a raging fire and a deep desire for learning and discovery. It just goes to show how even simple online platforms can ignite a passion in the simplest person to discover their true passions.

A certain something often serves a role only onceall before it becomes an empty vessel people tend to overlook. Something that no longer holds worth, and something that should be left aside like discarded trash (literally).

A perfect example of that certain something, is plastican item that comes in many forms, and mostif not all of it, is often seen littered like confetti. From the streets, parks, and any area you could nameyou'd see it disposed ever so sloppily, overcrowded in poorly segregated trash cans and all.

Be it as it is, what many saw as useless scrap, two people saw as a golden opportunity. Zean Agatha Zamora and Ewan Orencillo, Grade 10 and 11 representatives of Marcial O. Raola Memorial School's (MORMS') Supreme Secondary Learner Government (SSLG)strived to make their platforms a realitynow named Project TRASH (Transforming Resources and Segregating Helps).

In an interview with Rachelle Bagangan, the outgoing president of MORMS SSLG, she said that the preparation of the project's implementation took around two weeks, half spent on proposing, studying, and evaluating the benefits of the projectwhile the other half was spent on gathering the materials needed to craft the nets. She emphasized the importance of identifying the feasibility of the project, ensuring that what they'll execute is something that will benefit the school and its students in the long run.

The project aimed to provide a sustainable system where cleanliness is practiced, and where discipline is rewarded. Tall, green nets were placed all around the school so that students can dispose of polyethylene terephthalate (PET) bottles. SSLG, with the collaboration of the Boy Scouts of the Philippines (BSP), would collect these bottles twice a weekthen evaluate the quality of those that can still be sold before taking them to junk shops.

Bagangan also said that they wanted to enact this project in a way that was most entertaining to students. Instead of offering mere trash cans, they ultimately decided to use the aforementioned tall nets to simulate a shooting game. So that at least, in her words, the students could find entertainment in something that would help the school's environment.

Not only did they create a system that caters to the environment, they have created a system that gives back to the community.

Project TRASH was implemented back in August, 2025marking five months since its enactment. Despite the short time, Bagangan shared how much the project has already impacted the school for the better. The funds that were collected from selling PET bottles was used for MORMS' gift giving.

Furthermore, the income was spent beyond the schools borders, used to provide free school supplies and hygiene kits for the indigenous children in Malabnig.

Many seem to miss the hidden gem that lies deep beneath what many would see as mere rubble. But, with a careful eyeand a touch of creativity, what was once a mere idea had become a system that hits two birds with one stone.

Cultivating rich pasture for wanting sheep

By: Francine J. Orzales

Arid clay is hard to mold into rich pasturesit may take a while, but we'll get there: somehow, some day. Even from the smallest drops of water, and with the sincerest touches of care. This very principle applies with the goal Marcial O. Raola Memorial School (MORMS): to provide a sustainable haven of nutrition for each and every student, that just like sheepyearn a bountiful land that'd nourish them ever so generously.

Arid clay is hard to mold into rich pasturesit may take a while, but we'll get there: somehow, some day. Even from the smallest drops of water, and with the sincerest touches of care. This very principle applies with the goal Marcial O. Raola Memorial School (MORMS): to provide a sustainable haven of nutrition for each and every student, that just like sheepyearn a bountiful land that'd nourish them ever so generously.

According to Julie Millete, the former coordinator of Gulayan sa Paaralan Program (GPP)project GrOOVe: Growing Ornaments, Orchards, and Vegetables was born under the gulayan as mandated by the Department of Education (DepEd). Implemented back in 2021this four-year-long journey has helped specific minorities in the school: students who do not reach the normal BMI requirement.

It may not be there yet, but certainlythere's a good start. Though it had only been four years, its impact in the school's community is certainly visible. With the vegetables and other goodies provided by GPP, feeding programs hosted by the school are done with efficiencyhaving been consistent after the pinnacle of the dreaded pandemic.

From the records by the school nurse, Celine Mima30 out of the 50 students were able to complete the feeding program, and 29 of them showed improvement, from wasted and severely wastednow to normal in the previous school year.

Millete, along with the current coordinator of GPP, Marvi Fajardo, also explained how the harvest from the gulayan are used by the beneficiaries aside from feeding programs. The herbs and spices from the garden are used as seasoning for the snacks provided for the students, the vegetables found in the garden are also used as ingredients for TLE studentsand the other goodies are usually packed along with meat. Other goodies from the garden are also packed alongside meat so that students may bring it home and cook it at any timemostly done every class disruption or suspension.

Fajardo also highlighted that rooting out hunger is not the only beneficial impact the program provides. Students are actively encouraged to help cater the plants, even doing voluntary workas well as implementing their own sustainable gardens back at home.

Ultimately however, MORMS' intentions with the project is similar with United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Specifically, Zero Hunger. A world where food insecurity is the last thing people would think of, a world that feeds not only the people of today, but the generations that are bound to follow. In addition to the latter, MORMS also believes in satisfying not only the students' hunger for knowledge, but for a meal or two as well.

What was once arid clay now had small patches of timid grass. It is true, Fajardo admits that the gulayan has not been used to its maximum despite it having been expanded. The four hectares of free land has yet to be completely utilized. But with the help of consistency and perseverancethese small patches will eventually become abundant with time.

Marvi Fajardo had already made an initiative, expressing her will to collaborate with MORMS' Supreme Secondary Learner Government (SSLG) so that GPP's goals can reach every student. So that every student may learn that the gulayan's purpose is beyond a simple lesson in agriculture, or a humble pantry for seasoning; so that they may learn the importance of cultivating a sustainable haven where nourishment is its ever-budding fruit.

Final Thoughts

For now, what we have is but patches, short grass barely peaking through the cracks of now-moist clay. It may not seem like much, but that alone tells the massive effort that's been poured to give life to something so deserted. In the nick of time, what was lackadaisical will turn into bountand sure enough, wanting sheep will seek no longer of grass to chew: for there are many, in the rich pastures they reside in.
